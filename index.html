The Knights of Skirbeck

By Edward Evans

Located in Boston, Lincolnshire is the headquarters of the Knights of Skirbeck, a medieval re-enactment society who depict the life of a noble household in fifteenth century England, more specifically the reign of the House of Lancaster from 1399-1471.

Skirbeck HQ...

The group portray this particular period of time because the first Lancaster Monarch, King Henry IV, was born in Bolingbroke Castle, which is located just 15 miles from Boston.

After King Henry IV's death in 1413, his son Henry V ruled until 1422 and then his son succeeded the throne until 1471. See a timeline of their respective reigns below...

Although the group focus on this area of history, they do not re-enact specific events. They simply show what life was like for people living in a noble, military based house during this time.

Members of the group are also free to take on their own characters and re-enact other periods of time as long as they are authentic to the specific period and are executed to a high standard. This is something that they call ‘first-person living history’.

The Skirbeck house, which was formed in 2008 by three experienced re-enactors, now has 26 active members in total.

Their growth over the last decade has led to a lot of media attention in recent years. The group have appeared on the BBC’s The One Show and in films such as Gangsters, Guns and Zombies and Outlaw.

More recently, Noisey made a documentary about the group called ‘Big Narstie: The Real Game of Thrones’, in which British grime artist Big Narstie spends some time with the group and attends one of their shows. See the documentary below...

The reason behind Big Narstie’s visit to Boston is for him to live out his ‘Game of Thrones dreams’ of medieval nobility and warfare.

The Knights of Skirbeck are regularly linked with the immensely popular TV show ‘Game of Thrones’ and they accept and enjoy this, but they feel their connection to the show is very loose in theory.

Offence...
Arming Sword: The sword typical of the European High Middle Ages was a straight, double-edged weapon with a single-handed cruciform hilt and a blade length of about 70 to 80 centimetres. The type is frequently depicted in period artwork, and numerous examples have been preserved archaeologically.
Ahlspiess: A thrusting spear developed and used primarily in Germany and Austria from the 15th to 16th centuries. The ahlspiess consisted of a long thin spike of square cross section measuring up to about a metre (39 inches) in length, mounted to a round wooden shaft and sometimes secured with a pair of langets extending from the socket.
Dagger: A knife with a very sharp point and two or more sharp edges, typically designed or capable of being used as a thrusting or stabbing weapon. During this time, the dagger was often employed in the role of a secondary defense weapon in close combat. During the 14th century, it became fairly common for knights to fight on foot to strengthen the infantry defensive line. This necessitated greater dagger usage.

Defence...
Barbute helmet: A barbute (also termed a barbuta, which in Italian literally means "bearded", possibly because the beard of a wearer would be visible) is a visorless war helmet of 15th-century Italian design, often with a distinctive "T" shaped or "Y" shaped opening for the eyes and mouth.
Chainmail: A type of armour consisting of small metal rings linked together in a pattern to form a mesh. A coat of this armour is often referred to as a hauberk. By the 14th century, plate armour was commonly used to supplement mail. Eventually mail was supplanted by plate for the most part, as it provided greater protection against windlass crossbows, bludgeoning weapons, and lance charges.
Buckler: A small shield, up to 45 cm (up to 18 in) in diameter, gripped in the fist with a central handle behind the boss. While being used in Europe since antiquity, it became more common as a companion weapon in hand-to-hand combat during the Medieval and Renaissance periods.
Gauntlet: Historically, gauntlets were used by soldiers and knights. It was considered an important piece of armour, since the hands and arms were particularly vulnerable in hand-to-hand combat. Some medieval gauntlets had a built-in knuckle duster. When the hand was bunched into a fist, the backhand protection becomes pronounced from the fist just above the knuckles. This allowed the user to utilize the gauntlet as a melee weapon while still protecting the hand from damage when punching.
